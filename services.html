Math

  • Algebra I

    • Algebra is a broad category and is essentially using math in combination with unknowns. It is a large and foundational subject that typically spans multiple years, with a few years of pre-algebra in middleschool.

    In Algebra I you should already be comfortable with equations where you'll be expected to solve for \(x\) like this: \[\frac{x-2}{3} = 7\]

    You need to have a firm understanding of the order of operations (\(\text{PEMDAS}\)), be confident in the all of the properties of multiplication, division, addition, and subtraction, and understand that equations allow us to substitute one value for another: \[ \begin{align} \text{Let }\; \; b = 7\\ \\ \text{If }\; \; 3x + 1b &= 2\\ \\ 3x + 1(7) &= 2\\ 3x + 7 &=2\\ 3x &= -5\\ x &= \frac{-5}{3} \end{align} \]

    In Algebra I, a broad range of topics are introduced as a way to deepen your algebra and arithmetic skills while also broadening your mathematical horizons. The deepest studied will lines and their equations:

    \[y = \frac{x}{2} + 2\]

    Students in Algebra I will be expected to master many different aspects of working with lines, including: finding the slope, regression, finding perpendicular and parallel lines, and applications of lines. By the end of Algebra I, students will come to understand lines as more than simple equations; they are linear functions. The distinction between these terms should be meaningful as it's the bedrock for Algebra II.

    Aside from lines, students should be introduced to many other topics. The most common being:

    • inequalities
    • exponents
      \( \begin{align} y^4 &= y \cdot y \cdot y \cdot y \\ y^4 \cdot y^3 &= y^{4+3}\\ \frac{y^4}{y^2} &= y^4-2\\ y^{-1} &= \frac{1}{y}\\ y^0 &= 1\\ \end{align} \)
    • radicals
      \( \begin{align} \sqrt{x^2} &=x \\ \sqrt[n]{x^n} &=x \\ \sqrt{x} &= x^{\frac{1}{2}} \\ (\sqrt[3]{x})^2 &= x^{\frac{2}{3}} \\ \sqrt{72} = \sqrt{9\cdot 4 \cdot 2} &= 6 \sqrt{2} \end{align} \)
    • distribution
    • factoring
      \( \begin{align} 3x^2-\frac{x}{2} &=x(3x-\frac{1}{2})\\ x^2 + 3x -10 &= (x+5)(x-2)\\ 2x^2 + 7x + 3 &= (2x+1)(x+3) \end{align} \)
    • quadratic functions
  • Algebra II

    • To build upon the foundations of Algebra I, Algebra II centers on the abstraction of the function, essentially a relationship between two variables: the independent variable who's numberline is the horizontal axis and the dependent variable who's number line creates the vertical axis. For brevity, I will simply refer to these as the independent and dependendent.

    The independent represents something that changes of its own accord without influence from the dependendent. The common example is time, as the passage of time influences most phenomenon, but no phenomenon influences the passage of time. The dependendent is a value that that is entirely influenced by the independent. The common example is distance traveled, for example, consider figure 1. The \(t\)-axis represents the hours past, while the \(d\)-axis represents the distance traveled by a car travling in a straight line

    Using data points, key parts of the relationship can be determined: such as the rate of change (slope). By doing this, a function can be created that models the relationship. Using this function, we could now estimate/predict other values. This process of creating a curve from points is called regression, and--to me-- is at the core algebra II.

    Algebra II seeks to increase a student's profficiency in describing functions. By examining a function's formula and graph, a student's will learn to determine their properties like concavity, domain, range, and whether they're increasing or decreasing over various intervals. To introduce and hone algebra skills, students are introduced to two new parent functions: exponential and logorithmic functions. These functions can be applied in a variety of applied contexts--exponentials especially in financial and biological sciences.
